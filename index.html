Portfolio Strengths
1. Non-Sovereign Scaling in India — 9 Non-Sovereign transactions through Piramal, Adani, ReNew, Serentica, and Shriram have established a replicable model. The Non-Sovereign (NS) share crossed 50% in 2025 for the first time.
2. Bangladesh Onboarding — Bangladesh (joined 2021) has built a growing project pipeline, adding meaningful diversification to the founding-member concentration.
3. Climate Finance at 46.3% — Exceeds the 40% strategic target with 74 of 160 projects classified as climate-aligned. The 2025 cohort shows a strong acceleration in green lending, and Bangladesh's entire 5-project pipeline is climate-aligned.
4. Approval Speed — A median approval time of 9 months versus the World Bank's 18–24 months represents a structural institutional advantage in infrastructure financing.
Opportunities for Growth
1. Digital Infrastructure — Currently at 1 of 160 projects (0.6%), this sector represents a high-growth opportunity. Peer institutions such as the Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank (AIIB) and the World Bank allocate 5–10% of their portfolios to digital.
2. Local Currency Lending — At 23.1% against a 30% target, there is meaningful headroom, particularly in India (1 Indian Rupee loan from 42 projects), Brazil (no Brazilian Real loans from 35 projects), and South Africa.
3. Cancellation Rate — 18 projects (11.25%) have been cancelled. Strengthening early-stage pipeline screening would help improve this ratio over the next strategic cycle.
4. Portfolio Diversification — With China, India, and Brazil representing over 80% of active commitments, growing activity in Russia, South Africa, and newer members will further balance the portfolio across the full membership.
Strategic Partnership Opportunities
1. Brazil — Brazilian Real (BRL) Lending via BNDES — Co-financing with Banco Nacional de Desenvolvimento Econômico e Social (BNDES) and Banco de Desenvolvimento de Minas Gerais (BDMG) would unlock BRL-denominated instruments and grow the Non-Sovereign pipeline.
2. South Africa — Rand (ZAR) Lending via DBSA — A deeper partnership with the Development Bank of Southern Africa (DBSA) can accelerate South African Rand (ZAR) lending and open Non-Sovereign private-sector transactions.
3. India — Rupee (INR) Lending via NIIF and REC — Co-financing with the National Investment and Infrastructure Fund (NIIF) and Rural Electrification Corporation (REC) can enable Indian Rupee (INR) Non-Sovereign transactions at scale.
4. Bangladesh and Egypt — Cross-Member Corridors — Positioning newer members as co-financiers in multi-country projects would leverage Egypt's Middle East and North Africa (MENA) corridor alongside Bangladesh's South Asia infrastructure pipeline.
